Filter me this

What if we play with the frequency response of the negative feedback? We get filters. These are different from the filters we experimented with in Unit 3 and 4 because they are active filters. The filters we built earlier were passive filters. That means that all they did was reduce voltage. They reduced it more at some frequencies than others but they actually reduced the voltage (at least some) at all frequencies.

With op-amps we can easily create active filters. The circuit shown in figure 6-1 starts with and R1 and R2 value that give it a gain of one, so we get the same voltage in as out. A gain of one is referred to as unity gain. The addition of C1 means that high frequencies will pass through C1 (because it is an easier path, less reactance) so that:

  • There is more negative feedback for higher frequencies.
    • More feedback means less gain.
  • There is less than unity gain for higher frequencies.
  • There is unity gain for lower frequencies.

Though the example circuit has a gain of one for the lower frequencies, you can choose values for R1 and R2 to create any gain you want and still have less gain at higher frequencies.

The circuit shown in figure 6-1 is very common. Op-amps are very versatile, in fact you can often use the same op-amp for an audio circuit that you use for a radio frequency circuit. That way the manufacturer can sell more of them and the cost comes down. That also means that the op-amp can pass frequencies way above audio. There are actually a lot of those "higher than audio" frequencies floating around and they tend to get into audio equipment. They then get amplified and cause problems. By adding C1 and making it a small value (typically in the pF range) we can filter out anything above audio frequencies.

Filter me that

As you hopefully remember from units 3 and 4 we can use an inductor and get the opposite filter. The same is true with active op-amp filters. Figure 6-2 shows a high pass filter created with an inductor in parallel with R2.

  • There is more negative feedback for lower frequencies.
    • More feedback means less gain.
  • There is less than unity gain for lower frequencies.
  • There is unity gain for higher frequencies.

 

Both of these options still create simple first order filters. It is possible to create higher order filters and filters where the slope is adjustable. Keep reading to find out more.
