more than OpAmps

Though OpAmps are very common in audio circuits and also used in other applications they are not the only integrated circuits. A few others:

  • Voltage Regulators: Though these look like transistors, with just three connections, they are actually integrated circuits including at least a zener diode and resistor. In many cases they also include a transistor.
  • Specialty Audio circuits: This can be anything from a microphone preamplifier to a analog to digital converter.
  • DC-DC Converters: These allow the conversion of one DC voltage to another without much loss. This ends up being similar to the way that a transformer can convert one AC voltage into another without much loss.
  • Computers: Computers are full or processors, memory, and I/O circuits built from I.C.s.

How Do I Know What It Is?

I.C.s come in standardized sizes and shapes, the only way to know what is inside is to look up the part number and find the datasheet. A datasheet will give you all the information needed to design a circuit based on the I.C. This would include:

  • What the circuit does.
  • Maximum ratings (for instance how much voltage you can supply without it blowing up).
  • Noise, distortion, and any other specifications of the circuit.
  • Pin connections (pin 1 is not always the same thing).
  • Example circuits (sometimes you get these, though not always)

Here are some sample datasheet:

  • NE5532: The dual OpAmp you will use in lab.
  • NE5534: A single OpAmp version of the 5532
  • TL071, TL072, TL074: A family of single-quad OpAmps with JFET inputs for very high input impedance.
  • LM386: The power amplifier that is within your lab kit to power the speaker.
  • THAT1512: The microphone preamp that you will experiment with in later labs.
  • THAT4301: A dynamics engine that a compressor can be built upon.
