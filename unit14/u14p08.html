In this section I will present some other uses of OpAmps. These circuits are intended to give you a general idea of how some of these circuits might look. There are many variations of each of these circuits.

Balanced Input

One common use of OpAmps is for balanced inputs. In professional audio we use balanced circuits for most of our audio devices. The purpose of the balanced line is to reduce noise that would be added to the signal as it is sent through wires. More on this in the next course, A212 or A522.

Because OpAmps are differential devices (the output is the difference of the + and - inputs) they work very well for this. figure 8-1 shows and example of a balanced input amplifier.

  • R1 is the typical R1 from out inverting amplifier circuit.
  • R3 is serving the function of our typical R2. R1 and R3 set the gain of the circuit.
  • C1 is there to create a low pass filter that reduces anything above audio frequencies.
  • R2,R4, and C2 are there to match the impedance to ground of the Hot and Cold inputs. This is extremely important in determining how well a balanced line rejects external noise.

Summing

One of the things we do a lot in audio is mix signals together. The last circuit subtracted the inverting input from the non-inverting input. Figure 8-2 is a summing amplifier so it will add the signals at inputs 1-4 together. A mixing console would have a number of these circuits.

  • R1 through R4 are in place of the typical R1 in our inverting amplifier circuit.
  • R5 is in place of the typical R2.
  • Gain for input 1 (for example) would be $Gain = \frac {R5}{R1}$
  • Gain of each input could be set differently by changing R1-4.
  • C1 is there to create a low pass filter that reduces anything above audio frequencies.

Comparing

Figure 8-3 shows a window comparator circuit. This circuit would light the LED whenever the audio signal is within a certain range. This LED would turn off if the signal was too loud or too soft. This circuit is not something you will likely see, but demonstrates other possibilities with OpAmps.

  • The voltage divider of R1 and R2 sets the most positive voltage below which the LED will be turned on.
  • The voltage divider of R3 and R4 sets the most negative voltage above which the LED will be turned on.
  • The outputs of IC1 and IC2 are combined to bias the base of Q1 to turn the LED on or off.
