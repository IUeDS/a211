In unit 8 we looked at power supplies. The circuits we looked at were linear power supplies and were the only option for a long time. There is another type of power supply called a switch mode power supply ; these now see widespread use in all areas of electronics. Though the audio industry was slow to embrace them, they are now becoming common for audio use.

The big difference between linear and switching power supplies is efficiency. Linear power supplies waste a lot of energy. Switching power supplies are far more efficient, wasting less energy. That not only makes them use less electricity, but it also makes them physically smaller and less heavy.

The Old Way

Way back in unit 8 when voltage regulation was discussed, an I.C. version of a voltage regulator was briefly mentioned. Since this was before Op-Amps or transistors it could not be discussed in detail. These "three-legged" voltage regulators (as shown in figure 10-1) are very common in electronics equipment. Figure 10-2 shows a simplified schematic for one of these voltage regulators.
The input voltage can be reduced to provide a lower regulated voltage at the output. The regulator will use some of the voltage, so there needs to be more at the input than the desired output. The output voltage must be the same polarity as the input. For instance, if you want a negative voltage output you must have a negative voltage input (as well as a negative voltage regulator).

  • The output voltage is compared to a reference voltage by the op-amp.
    • Remember that the op-amp will output the difference between + and - inputs.
  • The output of the op-amp controls the Darlington pair transistors to set the output voltage.

This works very well and was the way most power supplies were built prior to the year 2000. Since then more and more electronic devices utilize switching regulators.

Let's save energy!

Modern electronic devices are not only smaller but they are more efficient, less of the electricity that they take in is wasted. One of the ways this is done is through the use of switch mode power supplies (SMPS). I will give an overview of the concept behind the switch mode power supply but not get into great detail.
The switch mode power supply concept can be a complete power supply or just a voltage regulator. There are also components called DC-DC converters that utilize the same concept.

Let’s take a standard microphone preamplifier using I.Cs. In most cases it would require three voltages: +15, -15, and +48. You can purchase a relatively inexpensive +48V power supply, like the one shown in figure 13-17. From this we could use a "three-legged" voltage regulator such as an LM317 to reduce that +48VDC to +15VDC. That extra 33V, however, would be converted to heat and (since we are not building a heater) be wasted. There is also no way to get -15V using a simple I.C. voltage regulator.
If we use a DC-DC converter, however, we can input +48VDC and get +15VDC and -15VDC out. The I.C. doesn’t waste the extra voltage; instead it simply takes what it needs. Switch mode regulators and power supplies can have efficiencies of 90% or more. That means that only 10% of the energy is wasted. In our I.C. example above that wasted 33V meant an efficiency of less than 32% as it wasted 68% of the energy.

How it works

With a switch mode regulator (or power supply) a transistor is used to switch the input DC on and off. This is typically done with a MOSFET and a high frequency oscillator. This oscillator should be operating above the range of human hearing and the sample rate of digital audio converters. A pulse-width-modulated square wave is created by the oscillator. The width of the “on” portion of the square wave determines how much of the voltage gets converted. (figure 13-18)

  • If the transistor is on for shorter amount of time the output voltage will be lower. (figure 13-19)
  • The less time the transistor is turned on, the less energy the circuit uses.

Figure 13-17 and 13-18 show simplified schematics to give an idea of how these circuits work. In these figures we simply have a function generator turning the FET on and off. In a real circuit, the FET would be controlled by a circuit that was measuring the output voltage and comparing that to a reference.
