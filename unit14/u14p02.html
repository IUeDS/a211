Operation Anyone?

The most common integrated circuit used in audio electronics is the operational amplifier, or Op-amp for short. The schematic symbol is shown in figure 2-1. Operational amplifiers got the start with the first computers. Originally these were build with tubes but later were replaced with transistor based op-amps. Whether tube or transistor they were designed to be building blocks that could be used for different mathematical operations. Addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and logarithmic operations could all be accomplished with an op-amp. The design of the op-amp circuit was such that the only changes needed for the different functions were where the input was (or inputs were) connected and how much feedback was used.

The modern op-amps we use for audio are still based on this original concept. Depending on where we connect the input(s) and how much feedback we allow we can create:

Gain?
Remember that gain refers to $\frac {Output V}{Input V}$.

  • amplifiers with gains from 1 up
  • "amplifiers" with gains of less than 1
  • summing amplifiers that combine signals
  • differential amplifiers
  • filter circuits

Looking at figure 2-1 you will see that there are five connections to the op-amp.

  • There are two input connections.
    • The Inverting Input: Signals coming into this input will have the opposite polarity at the output. ("-" on the schematic symbol)
    • The Non-Inverting Input: Signals coming into this input will have the same polarity at the output. ("+" on the schematic symbol)
  • There are two voltage connections.
    • One of these is for a positive voltage.
    • The other for a negative voltage or ground.
  • There is an output connection.

Gain

Operational amplifiers are designed to have much more gain than is required by most circuits. Not only is this gain extremely high, it is also unstable. Typical OpAmps will have a gain of around 1000, making the output 1000 times larger than the input. This is done to allow gain to be set (to less than the maximum) by adding negative feedback. This negative feedback, like the negative feedback discussed in chapter 6, also reduces distortion as it reduces gain.
It is important to realize that the output of an OpAmp is limited by the supply voltages. A typical OpAmp used for audio would have supply voltages of +15V and -15V. That means that the output, no matter how high the gain, would be limited to no more than 30VPK. In reality it would be even less than that as some voltage is used up by the OpAmp itself. With no negative feedback an OpAmp given a sine wave as an input will create a square wave output. That translates to a very distorted output.

Negative Feedback
More negative feedback = Less gain
Less negative feedback = More gain

Negative feedback is very easy to add. Simply connect a resistor from the output to the inverting input. An example basic circuit is shown in figure 2-2. In this example the feedback resistor is R2.

  • If R2 is made smaller we send more negative feedback to the amplifier and the gain (and voltage at the output) is reduced.
  • If R2 is made larger we send less negative feedback to the amplifier and the gain (and voltage at the output) is increased.

There are two points in the circuit shown in figure 2-2 that are not connected to anything nor are they labeled. The left side of R1 and the non-inverting (+) input are both not connected. These will either be the input to the circuit or connected to ground. Which one goes where determines whether it is an inverting or non-inverting amplifier. These will be discussed on the next two pages.

OpAmp Uses

Open a modern piece of audio equipment and you will likely see many OpAmps. That’s because they can be used for just about everything such as microphone preamplifiers, balanced inputs, equalizers, summing amplifiers, output amplifiers.
