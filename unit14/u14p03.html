Flippin Waveform

The first basic application of an op-amp we will look at is the inverting amplifier, shown in figure 3-1.The output polarity of this amplifier is opposite from the input. This may seem like a problem with using the amplifier but it is easily corrected with a second inverting amplifier circuit.

One of the benefits of this amplifier circuit over the non-inverting amplifier circuit (it's coming on the next page) is that it can have a gain of less than one.
Why would I want a gain of less than one? That means less gets out than comes in.
There are times when a gain of less than one is desirable. Even though you could reduce the voltage with resistors, the op-amp isolates the input form the output which is often very useful.

Gain

Gain in this circuit is very easy to find with some not too complex math.
$Gain = \frac {R2}{R1}$ Just make sure that R1 and R2 are the in the same units. (both in ohms or kilohms)

Some examples

  • A circuit with an R2 value of 10kΩ and R1 of 10kΩ:
    • would have a gain of 1. ($ \frac {10kΩ}{10kΩ}$)
    • gain of 1 means that the input and output are the same voltage
    • why would you want this? It isolates what is connected to the input from what is connected to the output.
  • A circuit with an R2 value of 10kΩ and R1 of 5kΩ:
    • would have a gain of 2. ($ \frac {10kΩ}{5kΩ}$)
    • gain of 2 means that output will be twice the voltage of the input
