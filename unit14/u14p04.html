Out Like the In

If you don't want to invert the polarity of the signal then you can connect the op-amp as a non-inverting amplifier. This circuit is shown in figure 4-1. In this case there is a slightly different gain formula, you add 1 to the inverting amplifier gain formula. This means that you can't get a gain of less than one. Note that R1 and R2 are in exactly the same place. All that has changed is that ground and input have swapped places, everything else stays the same.

Gain

Gain is again found with some simple math.
$Gain = 1 + \frac {R2}{R1}$ Just make sure that R1 and R2 are the in the same units. (both in ohms or kilohms)

Some examples

  • A circuit with an R2 value of 10kΩ and R1 of 10kΩ:
    • would have a gain of 2. ($1 + \frac {10kΩ}{10kΩ}$)
    • gain of 2 means that output will be twice the voltage of the input
  • A circuit with an R2 value of 10kΩ and R1 of 5kΩ:
    • would have a gain of 3. ($1 + \frac {10kΩ}{5kΩ}$)
    • gain of 3 means that output will be three times the voltage of the input
