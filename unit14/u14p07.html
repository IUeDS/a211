Slippery slope?

Like the passive filters from units 3 and 4, the active filters from page 6 are first order filters. Because of that they have a slope of 6dB/octave (as discussed in unit 4). It is possible to create higher order filters, and a steeper slope. One of the most common examples of this is the butterworth filter. A low pass butterworth filter is shown in figure 7-1. The slope of this filter is set by the ratio of the value of the two capacitors.

The component values in this circuit allow you to set:

  • Cutoff Frequency (fc): Cutoff frequency is the frequency where the filter starts to have an effect. In this case when the high frequencies are cut by -3dB. R1 and R2 are the same value, R in the formula is the value one of the resistors.
    $f_{c} = \frac {1}{2\pi R \sqrt{C_{1}C_{2}}} $

  • Filter Q: Filter Q, also called Q factor (Quality factor). Q indicates how wide of a range around the cutoff frequency is affected by the filter.
    • A high Q factor indicates that the filter affects a smaller area of frequencies around fC.
    • A low Q factor Â indicates that the filter affects a larger area of frequencies around fC.
    • Q is the inverse of bandwidth.
    • $Q = \frac {1}{2} \sqrt{\frac {C_{2}}{C_{1}}}$

    High Pass, you pass, everybody pass?

    You can also create a high pass butterworth filter, as shown in figure 7-2. Again we can adjust this circuit by changing component values:

    • Cutoff Frequency (fc): Cutoff frequency is the frequency where the filter starts to have an effect. In this case when the high frequencies are cut by -3dB. C1 and C2 are the same value, C in the formula is the value one of the capacitors.
      $f_{c} = \frac {1}{2\pi C \sqrt{R_{1}R_{2}}} $

    • Filter Q: Filter Q, also called Q factor (Quality factor). Q indicates how wide of a range around the cutoff frequency is affected by the filter.
      $Q = \frac {1}{2} \sqrt{\frac {R_{1}}{R_{2}}}$
