Step 3: What do I already know?

The third step will take you the farthest in understanding the circuit. This is where you look for portions of the circuit that you already understand. .

3a: Create a block diagram

You want to break down complicated scheamtics into sections. You are creating a block diagram. In some cases this will already be done for you on the schematic. figure 4-1 shows the full 1176 schematic. Note that the schematic (click on it to make it large enough to read) is divided into sections that are labeled: "T" Attenuator, Signal Pre Amp, Signal Line Amp, GR Meter Amp andGR Control Amp.

These are the sections of the circuit and would be the parts of the block diagram. You may be able to find a block diagram for your circuit or you may have to create one. A simplified block diagram for the 1176 is shown in figure 4-2.

If the block diagram is easy to create or you can find it already created then add it to your project now. If no, then just wait and create it later.

3b: Identify sub-circuits

Next you will identify portions of the circuit that are examples of the circuits we have already investigated in earlier courses or earlier in this couse.. These are called sub-circuits and will be circuits we have looked at in earlier courses or earlier in this course. They may be as simple as a series capacitor "that blocks DC while allowing AC(audio) to pass" or a more complex circuit like an NPN common emitter amplifier.

Once you have identified a sub-circuit that you have already studied, you can identify components that match components from the typical version of the sub-circuit. In most cases that will allow you to identify the function of at least a few of those components. Since the goal of this exercise is to define the function of every component, this step can get you very far along toward that goal.

The Process:

Look for portions of the circuit that are familiar. It is common for sub-circuits to be tied to active components so look around each active device (transistor, op-amp, or vacuum tube).

  1. Identify sub circuit amplifier type (common emitter, non-inverting, etc.).
  2. Identify components in those known circuits whose function is known from the typical sub-circuit.
    • match the component to the "typical" component. (R27 is in place of a typical R1)
    • list the component function.
  3. Identify and mark components that are not typical for the sub-circuit.
  4. Identify components missing from typical sub-circuit and their function (something else must be providing this).
  5. identify and list circuit needs not being met because of missing components (the base is not biased)

An Example

Here is a subcircuit in the 1176 signal preamplifier portion of the 1176 circuit. I have highlighted it on the schematic and color coded the components.

  1. This circuit has the input (from the collector of Q2) to the base and the output (to the base of Q4) coming from the collector. That makes it a common emitter amplifier.
  2. Components in their "typical" spot:
    • R18:
      • In the place of the typical R3
      • Function is to convert the changing current through the transistor into a changing voltage for the output.
    • R20:
      • In the place of the typical R4
      • Function is to avert thermal runaway
        • Without typical R4 current increases as the transistor heats up, causing more heat, causing more current, more heat….
        • With R4 in the circuit if current were to increase, then typical R4 causes voltage at emitter to increase which would decrease current through the transistor. 
        • The static current through the transistor (the current when there is no input signal) can not change from what was set by base DC bias once typical R4 is in place.
    • C8:
      • In the place of typical C3
      • Function is to give varying current (AC) a separate path from typical R3.
        • Low Reactance path creates no voltage drop, which means no change in emitter voltage.
        • Should be a large enough value to provide low reactance path for all audio frequencies.
        • If it is missing emitter voltage will change with varying current through typical R4 causing a reduction in amplification.
  3. Components that are not in typical:
    • R19
  4. Components that are missing compared to the typical sub-circuit
    • R1 and R2:
      • Act as a voltage divider to set the DC bias of the base.
        • Allows amplification of entire waveform.
          (This can also be adjusted to allow amplification of a specific portion of the waveform for Class AB or B circuits)
        • Sets 0V at input to halfway between saturation and cutoff so that there is equal room for amplification of the positive and negative halves of the input waveform.
      • R2 also affects the input impedance of the circuit.
    • C1:
      • Allows AC (input signal) to pass into circuit.
      • Blocks DC from getting into circuit and changing DC bias of base.
      • Block DC bias on base from getting out to what is connected to the input.
      • Typically a large enough value that all audio passes without voltage drop.
      • C1 is a part of the input impedance.
      • Can be left out when:
        • DC coupling is desired.
        • Other coupling method is used, such as a transformer.
    • C2:
      • Blocks DC that is in AC+DC at collector from going out the output.
      • Typically a large enough value that all audio passes without voltage drop.
      • C2 is a part of the output impedance.
      • Can be left out when:
        • DC coupling is desired.
        • Other coupling method is used, such as a transformer.
