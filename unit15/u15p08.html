First some back-GND

We are now going to investigate the microphone preamplifier (and line input) of the channel strip. It is worth making sure we understand what a microphone preamplifier does first. A microphone preamplifier must amplify signals from a microphone up to line level. For professional audio the microphone preamplifier must also have a balanced input. Condenser microphones also require the mic preamp to supply 48VDC to them through phantom power.

Microphone Level

Microphones convert acoustic energy (sound) into electrical energy. This electrical energy ends up being very small and we refer to the range of voltages that microphones produce as mic level. Mic level is typically described as being from -60dBu to -20dBu. We discussed dB in Unit 4 but what is a dBu? the "u" in dBu refers to the a reference voltage. In the case of dBu it is 0.775VRMS. You may also see dBV in which case the reference is 1VRMS. Professional audio uses dBu as the standard for measuring audio levels. Why 0.775VRMS is used is a discussion for the next semester course. for now just accept it :-).

The formula for dB (when dealing with voltage) is $dB = 20log(\frac {V1}{V2})$. Since we are using dBu, V2 in that equation becomes 0.775VRMS (It is important to keep VRMS in mind because V1 will also be in VRMS ). We can use that formula to define what voltage mic level would be. This is particularly useful if we want to test this circuit as we will need to know what voltage to send to the input.

We have a range of -60dBu to -20dBu.

First the low side of that range, -60dBu. Next the high side of that range, -20dBu.
$-60dBu = 20log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$
  1. divide -60 by 20 to get:
    $-3 = log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$
  2. convert the log equation to exponential to get:
    $10^{-3} = \frac {V1}{0.775Vrms}$
  3. covert the exponent to fraction to get:
    $\frac {1}{1000} = \frac {V1}{0.775Vrms}$
  4. multiple both sides by 0.775 to get:
    $\frac {0.775}{1000} = V1$
  5. solve what's left:
    $ V1 = \frac {0.775}{1000} = 0.000775Vrms$
  6. convert that to millivolts
    $ 0.000775Vrms = 0.775mVrms$
 $-20dBu = 20log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$
  1. divide -20 by 20 to get:
    $-1 = log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$
  2. convert the log equation to exponential to get:
    $10^{-1} = \frac {V1}{0.775Vrms}$
  3. covert the exponent to fraction to get:
    $\frac {1}{10} = \frac {V1}{0.775Vrms}$
  4. multiple both sides by 0.775 to get:
    $\frac {0.775}{10} = V1$
  5. solve what's left:
    $ V1 = \frac {0.775}{10} = 0.0775Vrms$
  6. convert that to millivolts
    $ 0.0775Vrms = 77.5mVrms$

We can now see (after all that math) that mic level ranges from 0.0775mVRMS to 77.5mVrms.

A microphone preamplifier must amplify that mic level signal up to line level. For professional audio, line level has defined nominal level of +4dBu. We will want to amplify that -60dBu to -20dBu signal up to +4dBu. Let's see what that means in terms of voltage, same math.

$4dBu = 20log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$

  1. divide 4 by 20 to get:
    $\frac {4}{20} = log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$
  2. convert the fraction to a decimal (to make the next step easier)
    $0.2 = log(\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms})$
  3. convert the log equation to exponential to get:
    $10^{0.2} =\frac {V1}{0.775Vrms}$
  4. multiple both sides by 0.775 to get:
    $10^{0.2} * 0.775 = V1$
  5. solve what's left (and round):
    $ V1 = 10^{0.2} * 0.775= 1.23Vrms$

So our preamplifier will be amplifying something that is between 0.000775VRMS and 0.775VRMS up to be 1.23VRMS.

If we use the gain formula from unit 10 $\frac {Vout}{Vin}$ we get:
$\frac {1.23Vrms}{0.000775Vrms}$ = a maximum gain of 1587
and
$\frac {1.23Vrms}{0.775Vrms}$ = a minimum gain of 1.5

Balanced Input

Professional audio uses balanced lines in order to reduce noise that might be added to the audio signal as it is sent through wires. Later units will cover this in greater detail but a simple understanding will suffice for now. Balanced lines are differential, like OpAmps. that means that the signal that we want to convey is the difference between the Hot and Cold lines. There are three connections used for most balanced lines, they are:

  • Hot: This connection carries the audio signal in its original polarity.
  • Cold: This connection carries a signal that is identical to Hot in every way except that it is opposite in polarity.
  • Ground: This connection is not required for all balanced lines but it is used for to reject additional noise and for phantom power. Phantom power is required by condenser microphones.

Balanced lines are differential, like OpAmps. that means that the signal that we want to convey is the difference between the Hot and Cold lines.

Phantom Power

Condenser microphones create an extremely low voltage from the diaphragm. This voltage is actually too low to travel down a cable to a preamplifier. Because of this, condenser microphones have amplifiers built into them to amplify the voltage from the diaphragm. This amplifier requires power and that power (for professional microphones at least ) is supplied from the preamplifier through phantom power. Phantom power is a DC voltage (+48V is typical though it can be lower) that is supplied to both the hot and cold connections of the microphone. This voltage is called phantom because it can be used when compared to ground, but to the audio path (which sees the difference between hot and cold) it doesn't exist because it is the same on hot and cold.

Most microphones using 48V phantom power will require about 3mA of current. The standard (defined by IEC) defines that there should be 14mA available.

Re-Cap

A microphone preamplifier needs to amplify the difference between Hot and Cold by as little as 1.5 and as much as 1587. It also needs to supply 48V phantom power to the microphone.
